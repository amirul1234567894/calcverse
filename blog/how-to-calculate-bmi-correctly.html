How to Calculate BMI Correctly: A Complete Guide

Body Mass Index (BMI) is one of the most widely used health screening tools in the world. Yet despite its simplicity, many people calculate it incorrectly or misinterpret their results. This guide walks you through everything you need to know — from the exact formula to what your number actually means.

What Is BMI?

BMI is a numerical value derived from your weight and height. Developed by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 19th century, it was originally a population-level statistical tool — not a clinical diagnostic measure. Today, it's used by doctors, public health agencies, and individuals worldwide as a quick first-pass assessment of weight status.

The World Health Organization (WHO) uses BMI as the standard measure for classifying underweight, overweight, and obesity across adult populations. It's cost-free, requires no equipment, and takes seconds to calculate — which explains its widespread adoption.

The BMI Formula

The formula is elegant in its simplicity:

BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ Height² (m)

In other words: take your weight in kilograms and divide it by your height in metres squared. That's it.

If you're using inches and pounds (imperial):

BMI = (Weight in lbs ÷ Height in inches²) × 703

BMI Categories Explained

The WHO defines four adult BMI categories:

  • Underweight: BMI below 18.5 — May indicate malnutrition, eating disorders, or underlying medical conditions. A doctor's evaluation is recommended.
  • Normal weight: BMI 18.5–24.9 — Associated with the lowest statistical risk of weight-related disease for most adults. This is the target range for most health guidelines.
  • Overweight: BMI 25–29.9 — Elevated risk of type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease. Lifestyle modifications are typically recommended.
  • Obese: BMI 30 and above — Significantly elevated risk of serious chronic conditions including heart disease, stroke, sleep apnoea, and certain cancers.

Some health organizations further divide obesity into Class I (30–34.9), Class II (35–39.9), and Class III (40+), with Class III sometimes called "severe" or "morbid" obesity.

Step-by-Step Examples

Example 1: Metric system

Person: 75 kg weight, 1.78 m tall

  • Step 1: Square the height → 1.78 × 1.78 = 3.1684
  • Step 2: Divide weight by squared height → 75 ÷ 3.1684 = 23.67
  • Result: BMI = 23.7 → Normal weight

Example 2: Imperial system

Person: 165 lbs weight, 5 ft 9 in (69 inches) tall

  • Step 1: Square the height → 69 × 69 = 4761
  • Step 2: Divide weight by squared height → 165 ÷ 4761 = 0.03466
  • Step 3: Multiply by 703 → 0.03466 × 703 = 24.36
  • Result: BMI = 24.4 → Normal weight

Limitations of BMI: When the Number Lies

BMI is a useful population-level screening tool, but it has well-documented limitations at the individual level:

  • Muscle vs fat: BMI cannot distinguish between lean muscle mass and body fat. A professional bodybuilder weighing 100 kg at 1.8 m has a BMI of 30.9 — technically "obese" — despite having very low body fat.
  • Age differences: Older adults tend to lose muscle mass and gain fat at the same BMI. A 65-year-old at BMI 22 may have more body fat than a 25-year-old at the same BMI.
  • Ethnicity: Research suggests that people of Asian descent face higher health risks at lower BMI values. Some health organizations use BMI 23+ as the overweight threshold for Asian populations.
  • Fat distribution: BMI ignores where fat is stored. Central (belly) fat is more metabolically harmful than fat stored in the hips and thighs, but both would register identically on a BMI scale.
  • Pregnancy: BMI calculations are meaningless during pregnancy as weight gain is expected and necessary.

For a more complete picture, doctors typically combine BMI with waist circumference measurement, blood pressure, blood glucose, and cholesterol levels.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the correct BMI formula?
The standard formula is: BMI = weight(kg) / height(m)². Divide weight in kilograms by the square of height in metres. For imperial: BMI = (weight in lbs / height in inches²) × 703.
What is a healthy BMI for a 40-year-old?
The standard healthy BMI range (18.5–24.9) applies to all adults regardless of age. However, some research suggests slightly higher BMI values (up to 26–27) may be acceptable for adults over 65 without increased mortality risk.
How often should I check my BMI?
Most health professionals recommend checking BMI every 6–12 months as part of a routine health review, or more frequently when actively managing weight.
Is BMI accurate for athletes?
No. Athletes with high muscle mass frequently have elevated BMI despite excellent metabolic health. If you're highly trained, body fat percentage (measured by DEXA scan or skinfold calipers) is a more meaningful metric.
